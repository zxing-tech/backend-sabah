THOUGHT RECORDS — A CORE CBT TECHNIQUE

A thought record is a structured worksheet that helps people examine and reframe unhelpful thoughts. It is one of the most widely used and evidence-based CBT tools.

THE 7-COLUMN THOUGHT RECORD

Column 1: SITUATION
What happened? Where were you? Who were you with? When was it?
Keep it factual and specific — like describing a scene in a movie.
Example: "Tuesday morning, my manager asked to see me in her office."

Column 2: AUTOMATIC THOUGHT
What went through your mind at that moment? What were you thinking?
Capture the exact thought, including images or memories.
Example: "She's going to tell me I'm not performing well. I might get fired."

Column 3: EMOTIONS
What emotions did you feel? Rate each one from 0-100%.
Example: "Anxiety (85%), Dread (70%), Shame (40%)"

Column 4: EVIDENCE THAT SUPPORTS THE THOUGHT
What facts support this automatic thought being true?
Only include observable facts, not feelings or interpretations.
Example: "She had a serious expression. I made an error in last week's report."

Column 5: EVIDENCE AGAINST THE THOUGHT
What facts contradict this automatic thought?
Example: "She's called me in before for positive feedback. My last performance review was good. One error doesn't usually lead to firing. She often looks serious — that's just her face."

Column 6: BALANCED/ALTERNATIVE THOUGHT
Based on all the evidence, what is a more realistic and balanced way to think about this?
This is NOT forced positivity — it's accuracy.
Example: "She might want to discuss anything — it doesn't have to be bad news. Even if she mentions the error, that's feedback, not a firing. My overall track record is solid."

Column 7: OUTCOME
How do you feel now? Re-rate your emotions from 0-100%.
Example: "Anxiety (45%), Dread (20%), Shame (15%)"

TIPS FOR EFFECTIVE THOUGHT RECORDS:
- Fill them out as close to the event as possible
- Be specific — vague situations lead to vague insights
- Distinguish between thoughts (words/images in your mind) and emotions (single-word feelings)
- The evidence columns are the most important — spend time here
- A balanced thought should feel believable, not dismissive
- Track patterns: do certain distortions show up repeatedly?
- Even small drops in emotional intensity (e.g., 85% to 65%) are meaningful progress

SIMPLIFIED 3-COLUMN VERSION (for beginners):
For people new to CBT, start with just three columns:
1. Situation — What happened?
2. Automatic Thought — What went through my mind?
3. Balanced Thought — What's another way to see this?

WHEN TO USE THOUGHT RECORDS:
- When experiencing strong negative emotions
- When noticing recurring negative thought patterns
- When feeling stuck in a negative loop
- As homework between therapy sessions
- Proactively before anxiety-triggering situations

COMMON OBSTACLES:
- "I can't find evidence against": Ask "What would a friend say?" or "What would I tell someone I care about?"
- "The balanced thought doesn't feel true": Remind that believing new thoughts takes practice — rate how much you believe it (0-100%) and track over time
- "I feel worse after filling it out": This can happen initially; it's part of the learning process. The skill improves with repetition.
