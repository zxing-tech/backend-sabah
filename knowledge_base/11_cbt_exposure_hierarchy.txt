GRADED EXPOSURE — CBT FOR ANXIETY AND AVOIDANCE

Graded exposure is a core CBT technique for anxiety disorders. It works by systematically and gradually facing feared situations, allowing the brain to learn that the feared outcome does not occur (or is manageable).

THE AVOIDANCE-ANXIETY CYCLE:
Feared Situation → Anxiety → Avoidance → Short-term Relief → Increased Fear of Situation → More Avoidance → ...

WHY AVOIDANCE MAKES ANXIETY WORSE:
- Avoidance prevents you from learning that you can cope
- Each avoidance reinforces the belief that the situation is dangerous
- The feared situation grows larger in your mind over time
- Avoidance often spreads to related situations (generalization)

HOW EXPOSURE WORKS:
- Facing feared situations in a controlled, gradual way
- Allows anxiety to naturally rise and then fall (habituation)
- Teaches the brain new safety associations (inhibitory learning)
- Builds confidence and self-efficacy
- Breaks the avoidance cycle

BUILDING A FEAR HIERARCHY (EXPOSURE LADDER):

Step 1: IDENTIFY THE FEARED SITUATION
What is the situation, object, or activity you're avoiding?
Example: Public speaking

Step 2: RATE YOUR FEAR (SUDS Scale)
SUDS = Subjective Units of Distress Scale (0-100)
0 = No anxiety at all
25 = Mild anxiety, can manage easily
50 = Moderate anxiety, uncomfortable but manageable
75 = High anxiety, very difficult
100 = Maximum anxiety, feels unbearable

Step 3: BUILD THE LADDER
List 8-12 situations related to your fear, from least to most anxiety-provoking.

Example — Fear of Public Speaking:
1. Reading about public speaking tips (SUDS: 10)
2. Practicing a speech alone at home (SUDS: 20)
3. Recording yourself giving a speech (SUDS: 30)
4. Giving a speech to one trusted friend (SUDS: 40)
5. Giving a speech to 2-3 close friends (SUDS: 50)
6. Speaking up in a small meeting at work (SUDS: 55)
7. Presenting to a small group of 5-6 colleagues (SUDS: 65)
8. Presenting to a medium group of 10-15 people (SUDS: 75)
9. Giving a presentation to a department of 20+ people (SUDS: 85)
10. Giving a keynote to a large audience (SUDS: 95)

Example — Social Anxiety:
1. Smile at a stranger while walking (SUDS: 15)
2. Make small talk with a cashier (SUDS: 25)
3. Call a friend on the phone (SUDS: 35)
4. Attend a small gathering with people you know (SUDS: 45)
5. Start a conversation with an acquaintance (SUDS: 55)
6. Attend a party where you know few people (SUDS: 65)
7. Join a new group activity or class (SUDS: 70)
8. Introduce yourself to a stranger at an event (SUDS: 80)
9. Host a small gathering (SUDS: 85)
10. Give a toast at a social event (SUDS: 90)

Step 4: START AT THE BOTTOM
Begin with the lowest item on your ladder. Stay in the situation until anxiety naturally decreases (usually 20-45 minutes, but can be shorter).

Step 5: REPEAT AND MOVE UP
Repeat each step until your SUDS rating drops significantly (by about half). Then move to the next step.

TYPES OF EXPOSURE:

1. IN VIVO (Real Life): Facing the actual feared situation
2. IMAGINAL: Vividly imagining the feared situation
3. INTEROCEPTIVE: Deliberately inducing feared physical sensations (e.g., spinning in a chair for dizziness fear)
4. VIRTUAL: Using technology to simulate feared situations

GUIDELINES FOR EFFECTIVE EXPOSURE:
- Gradual: Start easy and work up
- Prolonged: Stay in the situation long enough for anxiety to naturally decrease
- Repeated: Do each step multiple times
- Without safety behaviors: Resist subtle avoidance (e.g., checking phone, standing near exit)
- Planned: Schedule exposures in advance
- Reflected upon: After each exposure, note what you learned

WHAT TO EXPECT DURING EXPOSURE:
- Anxiety will rise initially — this is expected and normal
- Anxiety will peak and then naturally decrease (habituation curve)
- Early exposures may feel intense; later ones will feel easier
- Some days will be harder than others — this is normal
- Progress is not always linear

COMMON OBSTACLES:
- "I can't do it": Break the step down further. If "call a friend" is too hard, start with "text a friend."
- "It didn't work — I still felt anxious": Some anxiety is expected. Success is doing it, not feeling zero anxiety.
- "I did it but only because I had my phone/friend/escape plan": Gradually reduce safety behaviors as you repeat the step.
- "I had a setback": Setbacks are part of the process. Go back a step or two and rebuild.
