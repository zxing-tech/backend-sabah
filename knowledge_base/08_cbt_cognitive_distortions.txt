COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS — A CBT REFERENCE

Cognitive distortions are systematic errors in thinking that reinforce negative beliefs. Identifying them is the first step toward challenging and reframing unhelpful thoughts.

1. ALL-OR-NOTHING THINKING (Black-and-White Thinking)
Seeing things in only two categories rather than on a continuum.
Example: "If I don't get an A, I'm a total failure."
Challenge: "Is there a middle ground? A B+ still reflects strong effort and understanding."

2. CATASTROPHIZING (Magnification)
Expecting the worst possible outcome or blowing things out of proportion.
Example: "If I make a mistake at work, I'll definitely get fired."
Challenge: "What's the most likely outcome? Have I made mistakes before and kept my job?"

3. MIND-READING
Assuming you know what others are thinking without evidence.
Example: "Everyone at the party thought I was boring."
Challenge: "What evidence do I actually have? Did anyone say or do something specific?"

4. FORTUNE-TELLING
Predicting negative outcomes as if they are certain.
Example: "I know this date will go terribly."
Challenge: "Can I really predict the future? What are some other possible outcomes?"

5. OVERGENERALIZATION
Taking one instance and making a broad rule from it.
Example: "I failed this test, so I'll never pass any test."
Challenge: "Is this one event really a pattern? What about times I have succeeded?"

6. EMOTIONAL REASONING
Believing something is true because you feel it strongly.
Example: "I feel stupid, so I must be stupid."
Challenge: "Feelings aren't facts. What evidence contradicts this feeling?"

7. SHOULD STATEMENTS
Using 'should,' 'must,' or 'ought to' in ways that create unrealistic pressure.
Example: "I should be able to handle this without help."
Challenge: "Says who? Is this expectation realistic? Would I apply this rule to a friend?"

8. PERSONALIZATION
Taking excessive responsibility for events outside your control.
Example: "My friend seems upset — I must have done something wrong."
Challenge: "Could there be other explanations for their mood that have nothing to do with me?"

9. MENTAL FILTER (Selective Abstraction)
Focusing exclusively on one negative detail while ignoring the bigger picture.
Example: "My boss pointed out one typo, so my whole report was terrible."
Challenge: "What did they say about the rest of the report? What went well?"

10. DISCOUNTING THE POSITIVE
Dismissing positive experiences as not counting or as flukes.
Example: "They only said my presentation was good to be polite."
Challenge: "What if they genuinely meant it? What evidence supports that they were sincere?"

11. LABELING
Attaching a fixed, global label to yourself or others based on specific behavior.
Example: "I'm a loser" instead of "I didn't do well on this one task."
Challenge: "Am I defining my whole self by one event? Would I label a friend this way?"

12. JUMPING TO CONCLUSIONS
Making negative interpretations without supporting facts. Includes both mind-reading and fortune-telling.
Example: "She didn't text back, so she must be angry at me."
Challenge: "What are three other reasons she might not have texted back?"

HOW TO USE THIS IN SESSIONS:
- Help the client identify which distortion matches their automatic thought
- Normalize it: "This is a very common thinking pattern called ___"
- Use Socratic questioning to challenge the distortion
- Guide them to a more balanced, realistic thought
- Remind them: the goal is not positive thinking, but accurate thinking
