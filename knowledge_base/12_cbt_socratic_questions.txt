SOCRATIC QUESTIONING — THE HEART OF CBT

Socratic questioning is the primary method of guided discovery in CBT. Rather than telling someone their thought is wrong, you ask questions that help them examine and evaluate their own thinking. The goal is insight through self-discovery, not persuasion.

PRINCIPLES OF SOCRATIC QUESTIONING:
- Be genuinely curious, not leading or rhetorical
- Ask one question at a time
- Allow silence for reflection
- Follow the client's answers with further questions
- The goal is not to "win" but to help them think more flexibly
- Validate before questioning: acknowledge the emotion, then explore the thought

CATEGORIES OF SOCRATIC QUESTIONS:

1. EXAMINING THE EVIDENCE
Purpose: Help evaluate whether the thought is supported by facts.
- "What evidence supports this thought?"
- "What evidence goes against it?"
- "Are you basing this on facts or feelings?"
- "If a friend looked at this situation, what evidence might they see?"
- "What have you experienced in the past that relates to this?"

2. CONSIDERING ALTERNATIVES
Purpose: Open up other ways of seeing the situation.
- "Is there another way to look at this?"
- "What would you tell a close friend who had this thought?"
- "What are some other possible explanations?"
- "If you weren't feeling [anxious/sad/angry], how might you see this differently?"
- "Six months from now, how might you view this situation?"
- "What would [someone you respect] say about this?"

3. EXAMINING THE CONSEQUENCES
Purpose: Explore the impact of thinking this way.
- "What happens when you believe this thought?"
- "How does this thought affect your behavior? Your mood? Your relationships?"
- "What would change if you didn't believe this thought?"
- "Is this thought helping you or hurting you?"
- "What's the cost of holding onto this belief?"

4. TESTING ASSUMPTIONS
Purpose: Challenge underlying beliefs and rules.
- "What assumptions are you making here?"
- "Is this a fact or an opinion?"
- "Are you confusing a possibility with a certainty?"
- "What rule are you applying, and where did it come from?"
- "Does this rule apply to everyone, or just you?"

5. DECATASTROPHIZING
Purpose: Reduce the perceived severity of feared outcomes.
- "What's the worst that could happen?"
- "If the worst happened, how would you cope?"
- "What's the best that could happen?"
- "What's most likely to happen?"
- "Have you faced something like this before? How did you manage?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how bad would this really be?"

6. EVALUATING PROBABILITY
Purpose: Assess how likely the feared outcome actually is.
- "How likely is it that this will actually happen? (0-100%)"
- "How many times has this happened before out of all the times it could have?"
- "What usually happens in situations like this?"
- "Are you overestimating the likelihood because you're anxious?"

7. EXPLORING CORE BELIEFS
Purpose: Uncover deeper beliefs driving the automatic thought.
- "What does this situation mean about you?"
- "If that thought were true, what would that say about you as a person?"
- "Where did you first learn to think this way?"
- "Is there a pattern in how you see yourself across different situations?"
- "What would it mean to you if this thought turned out to be false?"

MATCHING QUESTIONS TO COMMON DISTORTIONS:

ALL-OR-NOTHING THINKING:
→ "Is there a middle ground between [extreme A] and [extreme B]?"
→ "Can you think of a time when the outcome was somewhere in between?"

CATASTROPHIZING:
→ "What's the most realistic outcome, not the worst case?"
→ "If it did happen, what would you actually do? How would you cope?"

MIND-READING:
→ "Do you know for certain what they were thinking?"
→ "What other reasons might explain their behavior?"

OVERGENERALIZATION:
→ "Is this one event really representative of everything?"
→ "Can you think of exceptions to this pattern?"

EMOTIONAL REASONING:
→ "Just because you feel [stupid/worthless/unlovable], does that make it true?"
→ "What would the evidence look like if we set the feeling aside?"

SHOULD STATEMENTS:
→ "Where does this 'should' come from?"
→ "Would you hold someone else to this same standard?"

PERSONALIZATION:
→ "What other factors might have contributed to this?"
→ "Is it possible this had nothing to do with you?"

TIPS FOR EFFECTIVE SOCRATIC QUESTIONING IN SESSIONS:
- Start with the thought: "What was going through your mind?"
- Validate first: "I can see why that thought would make you feel [emotion]."
- Then explore: "Let's look at this thought together..."
- Stay collaborative: "I'm curious about..." rather than "You should think..."
- Pace yourself: don't ask too many questions in succession
- Summarize insights: "So it sounds like you're realizing that..."
- Let the client do the work: resist the urge to provide the "right" answer
